## 1. Glossary (18 Terms)

Core terminology used across the app, content, and marketing. Definitions are written for two audiences: the internal team (precise) and the user-facing copy (plain English equivalents noted where different).

---

**Added Sugar**
Sugar that is added to food during processing or preparation — as opposed to sugar occurring naturally in whole foods (fruit, dairy). The WHO recommends limiting added sugar to <10% of daily calories (~50g / ~12 tsp for a 2,000 kcal diet). MyFitnessPal's inability to distinguish added from natural sugar is a known user frustration and a product opportunity.
_User-facing copy:_ "sugar that doesn't belong there"

---

**Blood Glucose / Blood Sugar**
The concentration of glucose in the bloodstream, measured in mg/dL (US) or mmol/L (international). Normal fasting range: 70–99 mg/dL. High added sugar intake causes repeated spikes and crashes — the physiological basis for "energy dips" and post-meal fatigue that users report.
_Relevance:_ The health timeline feature ("after 7 days, blood sugar stabilises") is grounded here.

---

**Insulin Spike / Insulin Resistance**
When blood glucose rises sharply, the pancreas releases insulin to bring it back down. Repeated spikes over time lead cells to become less responsive — insulin resistance — a precursor to type 2 diabetes. Pre-Diabetic Paul's A1C of 6.1 reflects accumulated insulin resistance.
_User-facing copy:_ "your body's sugar alarm going off too many times"

---

**HbA1c (Glycated Haemoglobin)**
A blood marker reflecting average blood glucose over 2–3 months. Normal: <5.7%; Pre-diabetic: 5.7–6.4%; Diabetic: ≥6.5%. The single most motivating number for users like Paul — it's measurable, medical, and changes with sustained dietary behaviour.
_Product note:_ A "90-day challenge" framed around A1C improvement maps directly onto the medical test cycle.

---

**Dopamine Pathway (Mesolimbic System)**
The brain's reward circuit, running from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the nucleus accumbens. Activated by pleasurable stimuli including food, drugs, and social connection. Sugar triggers dopamine release via this pathway — the neurological basis for craving, tolerance, and habit formation. Same circuit implicated in alcohol and drug dependence.
_Scientific basis for the app's "neuroscience" framing._

---

**Sugar Craving**
An intense, often sudden urge to consume sweet or high-carbohydrate foods, driven by a combination of: blood glucose dip, dopamine anticipation, learned habit cues (time of day, location, emotional state), and stress hormones (cortisol). Cravings typically peak at 15–20 minutes and subside if not acted upon.
_Product implication:_ The SOS session needs to last ~15 minutes to bridge the craving window.

---

**Withdrawal (Sugar)**
Physical and psychological symptoms occurring in the first 3–7 days of eliminating added sugar: headaches, fatigue, brain fog, irritability, and mood swings. Caused by the brain recalibrating dopamine sensitivity after prolonged sugar stimulation. Not medically dangerous (unlike alcohol withdrawal) but the most common reason users quit quitting.
_User-facing copy:_ "the hard days" — normalise and name them.

---

**Habit Loop (Cue → Routine → Reward)**
Charles Duhigg's model of habit formation (The Power of Habit, 2012), grounded in MIT basal ganglia research. A cue (3pm, work stress) triggers a routine (walking to the vending machine), which delivers a reward (dopamine hit from sugar). Breaking a habit requires substituting the routine while keeping the cue and reward structure.
_Product implication:_ The trigger logging and alternative suggestions features are direct applications of this model.

---

**Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)**
An evidence-based psychological intervention that targets the relationship between thoughts, feelings, and behaviours. In the context of eating behaviour, CBT techniques include: identifying automatic negative thoughts, cognitive restructuring, and behavioural experiments. Used by Sugarless (20-step Journey) and Noom. Effective for food-related behaviour change per multiple meta-analyses.
_Content strategy:_ Daily lessons should be explicitly CBT-adjacent without requiring clinical framing.

---

**Mindful Eating**
A practice derived from mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR), applied to food consumption: eating slowly, without distraction, with attention to hunger/fullness cues and sensory experience. Reduces binge eating and emotional eating in clinical studies. Distinguishes between physical hunger and emotional/habitual eating.
_App application:_ Pre-meal check-in prompts, post-meal reflection in the journal.

---

**Glycaemic Index (GI) / Glycaemic Load (GL)**
GI ranks foods by how quickly they raise blood glucose (scale 0–100; glucose = 100). GL adjusts for serving size. High-GI foods (white bread, 75; table sugar, 65; dates, 103) cause rapid blood sugar spikes. Low-GI alternatives (oats, 55; berries, 40; dark chocolate 85%+, 22) satisfy sweet cravings with less metabolic impact.
_"Eat this instead" engine:_ The food alternatives feature should be built on GI/GL data.

---

**Food Addiction**
A behavioural pattern characterised by compulsive consumption of highly palatable foods (typically high in sugar, fat, or both), loss of control over intake, and continued use despite negative consequences. Measured by the Yale Food Addiction Scale (YFAS). Not formally in DSM-5, but evidence base is growing. Overlaps with but is distinct from "sugar addiction."
_Regulatory note:_ The app should not claim to treat food addiction — use "reduce sugar habits" language.

---

**Urge Surfing**
A mindfulness technique developed by G. Alan Marlatt for addiction treatment: observing a craving with curiosity rather than acting on it or suppressing it, recognising that cravings are time-limited waves that rise and fall. Evidence-based for reducing relapse in substance use disorders and eating behaviour.
_SOS session technique:_ One of the AI's craving-management tools alongside alternatives and distraction.

---

**Emotional Eating**
Using food — typically high-sugar, high-fat comfort foods — to manage negative emotional states (stress, boredom, loneliness, anxiety) rather than physical hunger. Distinct from physical hunger: onset is sudden, craving is specific (chocolate, not broccoli), accompanied by emotional distress. The primary trigger pattern for Stress Sarah.
_Screening:_ Onboarding quiz should identify emotional eating as a primary pattern to personalise the AI responses.

---

**Harm Reduction**
A public health approach that accepts that complete abstinence is not always the goal; instead, it aims to reduce the negative consequences of behaviour. Applied to sugar: supporting "reduce by 50%" goals alongside "quit completely." Validated by Sunnyside (alcohol) — members reduce drinking by 30% on average.
_Product principle:_ Sugar Quit should explicitly support reduction goals, not just elimination. "Cut back" mode vs. "quit" mode.

---

**Relapse / Slip**
In behaviour change contexts, returning to an unwanted behaviour after a period of abstinence or reduction. Relapse is statistically normal — not a personal failure. The distinction between a slip (single episode) and a relapse (return to baseline pattern) is clinically important and poorly handled by current apps (streak resets punish both equally).
_Design principle:_ A slip should not reset the streak. The app should distinguish between a slip and a relapse and respond with support, not punishment.

---

**Neuroplasticity**
The brain's ability to rewire neural connections in response to repeated experience. In habit change contexts: new behaviour patterns, repeated consistently, physically strengthen alternative neural pathways over 60–90 days (Phillippa Lally, UCL, 2010 — average habit formation: 66 days, range 18–254).
_Content hook:_ "You are literally rewiring your brain" — a compelling and scientifically accurate framing for the 90-day curriculum.

---

**Cortisol**
The primary stress hormone, released by the adrenal glands in response to perceived threat or pressure. Cortisol directly stimulates sugar cravings by signalling the body to replenish fast-burning glucose. This is the physiological link between workplace stress and the 3pm candy bowl — not weakness, but biology.
_Messaging principle:_ Framing cravings as a cortisol response removes moral weight from the user. "Your biology is working against you. Here's how to work with it."

## 2. Scientific Sources

### Source 1 — Hoebel et al. (2008): Evidence for Sugar Addiction
**Citation:** Hoebel, B.G., Avena, N.M., Bocarsly, M.E., & Rada, P. (2008). Natural addiction: a behavioural and circuit model based on sugar addiction in rats. *Journal of Addiction Medicine*, 3(1), 33–41. PMC link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2235907/

**Key findings:**
- Rats given intermittent access to sugar (12h on, 12h off) showed binge-eating, escalating intake, and withdrawal symptoms (anxiety, teeth chattering) when sugar was removed
- Sugar causes dopamine and opioid release in the nucleus accumbens — the same reward pathway activated by cocaine and morphine
- Cross-sensitisation demonstrated: sugar-exposed rats showed heightened response to amphetamine
- Authors conclude: "the evidence supports the hypothesis that, under certain dietary conditions, rats can become sugar dependent"

**App relevance:** Scientific grounding for the "your brain is wired for sugar" content in the 90-day curriculum. Justifies neuroscience positioning without overclaiming clinical addiction.

---

### Source 2 — Avena et al. (2008): Sugar and Addiction — Biological Underpinnings
**Citation:** Avena, N.M., Rada, P., & Hoebel, B.G. (2008). Evidence for sugar addiction: behavioural and neurochemical effects of intermittent, excessive sugar intake. *Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews*, 32(1), 20–39. PMC link: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2235907/

**Key findings:**
- Sugar bingeing produces neurochemical changes parallel to drug addiction: dopamine release, opioid receptor upregulation, then downregulation with chronic exposure
- Binge–abstinence cycles increase dopamine in NAc on first hit (sensitisation)
- The neurological signature of sugar intake overlaps with the signature of opiates

**App relevance:** Foundation for "Day 1–7 is withdrawal, not weakness" messaging. Explains why the first week is the hardest and why it improves after day 7.

---

### Source 3 — Lally et al. (2010): How Habits Are Formed
**Citation:** Lally, P., van Jaarsveld, C.H.M., Potts, H.W.W., & Wardle, J. (2010). How are habits formed: modelling habit formation in the real world. *European Journal of Social Psychology*, 40(6), 998–1009. UCL/Wiley. https://doi.org/10.1002/ejsp.674

**Key findings:**
- Average time for a behaviour to become automatic: **66 days** (range: 18–254 days)
- Missing a single day did not significantly affect habit formation — recovery from a slip is fast
- Habit strength increases most rapidly in the first ~30 days, then slows

**App relevance:**
1. Justifies a 90-day curriculum (covers the full habit formation range for most people)
2. "Missing one day doesn't break the habit" is the scientific basis for the non-punitive slip design
3. The 30-day milestone is when habit formation decelerates — requires fresh motivation content at this point

---

### Source 4 — Linardon et al. (2020): CBT Apps for Eating Behaviour
**Citation:** Linardon, J., & Fuller-Tyszkiewicz, M. (2020). Smartphone-delivered interventions for eating disorders. *International Journal of Eating Disorders*, 53(5), 625–640. https://doi.org/10.1002/eat.23264

**Key findings:**
- Smartphone-delivered CBT interventions produce significant reductions in binge eating, emotional eating, and dietary restraint compared to control conditions
- Self-monitoring via apps (food logs, mood logs) combined with CBT techniques (cognitive restructuring, behavioural experiments) outperforms logging alone
- Engagement declines sharply after week 3 without active personalisation or coaching prompts

**App relevance:**
1. Validates that an app can deliver effective CBT-adjacent content (not just clinical face-to-face therapy)
2. The engagement cliff at week 3 is a critical retention design challenge — the AI must escalate personalisation after day 21
3. Combined monitoring + CBT is more effective than either alone — supports the journalling + AI coaching combo

---

### Source 5 — Kwasnicka et al. (2016): Mechanisms of Behaviour Change
**Citation:** Kwasnicka, D., Dombrowski, S.U., White, M., & Sniehotta, F. (2016). Theoretical explanations for maintenance of behaviour change: a systematic review of behaviour theories. *Health Psychology Review*, 10(3), 277–296. https://doi.org/10.1080/17437199.2016.1151372

**Key findings:**
- Five mechanisms predict long-term behaviour change maintenance: **motivation, self-regulation, psychological resources, habit, and environment**
- Social support significantly moderates maintenance — people with accountability partners maintain behaviour change longer
- Self-monitoring (tracking) alone predicts initiation, not maintenance; habit formation predicts maintenance

**App relevance:**
1. Community and accountability features are not "nice to have" — they are evidence-based maintenance mechanisms
2. Tracking features should be framed as habit-building tools, not scorecards
3. Environmental modification (pre-loading what to do in high-risk situations) addresses the environment mechanism directly — the social scripts feature for Chloe is scientifically grounded here

## 3. Regulatory Review & Disclaimers

### 3.1 What Sugar Quit Is (and Is Not)

Sugar Quit is a **wellness and behaviour change application**. It is NOT:
- A medical device (not FDA-regulated under 21 CFR Part 820)
- A clinical treatment for any eating disorder, addiction, or medical condition
- A substitute for professional medical, dietary, or psychological advice

This classification is intentional and protects the app from:
- FDA medical device registration requirements
- FTC health claim scrutiny
- App Store health app restrictions requiring clinical validation

### 3.2 Claims to Avoid (Regulatory Risk)

| Claim Type | Risky Version | Safe Version |
|-----------|---------------|--------------|
| Medical claims | "Treats sugar addiction" | "Helps build healthier sugar habits" |
| Diagnostic claims | "Identifies if you have food addiction" | "Helps you understand your relationship with sugar" |
| Outcome guarantees | "Lose X lbs in 30 days" | "Many users report reduced cravings within 7 days" |
| A1C claims | "Will lower your blood sugar" | "Reducing added sugar is associated with improved blood glucose markers" |
| Addiction treatment | "Sugar addiction recovery program" | "90-day behaviour change curriculum backed by neuroscience" |

### 3.3 Required Disclaimers

**In-app disclaimer (onboarding screen, required):**
> "Sugar Quit is a wellness app designed to support healthier eating habits. It is not a medical device, clinical treatment, or substitute for professional medical advice. If you have diabetes, pre-diabetes, an eating disorder, or any medical condition, please consult your doctor before making dietary changes."

**AI conversation disclaimer (shown in SOS session):**
> "The Sugar Quit AI provides general wellness support, not medical or psychological advice. If you are experiencing a mental health crisis, please contact a healthcare professional."

**Content disclaimer (curriculum lessons):**
> "Content is for informational purposes only and is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."

### 3.4 Eating Disorder Safeguarding

Apps in this category must handle users with or at risk of eating disorders (anorexia, orthorexia, bulimia, ARFID) responsibly.

**Required safeguards:**
- Onboarding screening question: "Have you ever been diagnosed with an eating disorder?" → If yes, show resources and recommend professional support before proceeding
- The AI must not reinforce extreme restriction language ("never eat sugar again," "sugar is poison")
- Calorie content must NOT be shown alongside sugar content — triggers restriction spiral in vulnerable users
- Include a "not for everyone" clause in onboarding: "This app focuses on reducing added sugar, not eliminating all sweetness from life"

### 3.5 GDPR / CCPA — Health Data

Sugar intake, craving logs, and mood data constitute **health-related personal data** under GDPR (EU) and CCPA (California). Required:
- Explicit opt-in consent to collect health data (not bundled with general T&C)
- Right to export and delete all personal data
- Data is not sold to third parties (explicit statement)
- Supabase row-level security (RLS) must be enabled — already in tech stack

### 3.6 App Store Health Category

Apple classifies apps in the "Health & Fitness" category. Sugar Quit should NOT position itself in "Medical" — this triggers manual review and clinical validation requirements. "Health & Fitness" → "Nutrition" subcategory is the correct placement.

## 4. Content Strategy

### 4.1 Positioning Voice

**Core voice principle:** Sugar Quit speaks like a knowledgeable, warm friend who happens to have a neuroscience degree — not like a doctor, a nutritionist, or a fitness influencer.

| Tone Attribute | What It Means in Copy |
|---------------|----------------------|
| Science-grounded | Reference real mechanisms (dopamine, cortisol, neuroplasticity) in plain language |
| Non-judgmental | Never imply the user is weak, lazy, or lacking willpower |
| Immediate | Address the now, not the abstract future ("in 30 days") |
| Specific | "Eat a handful of almonds" beats "try a healthy snack" |
| Honest | Acknowledge that this is hard; don't pretend it isn't |

---

### 4.2 The 90-Day Curriculum Structure

The curriculum is the primary retention mechanism. Based on Lally (2010): most habit formation completes by day 66; the curriculum runs to day 90 to cover the full range and add a buffer.

**Phase 1 — Foundation (Days 1–14): Understand your enemy**

| Day Range | Theme | Science Hook |
|-----------|-------|--------------|
| Days 1–3 | Why sugar feels like it controls you | Dopamine pathway, nucleus accumbens |
| Days 4–7 | The withdrawal week — what's happening in your brain | Dopamine recalibration, cortisol interaction |
| Days 8–10 | The hidden sugar map — where it actually is | Label reading, 60+ sugar names |
| Days 11–14 | Your personal trigger map | Habit loop (cue → routine → reward) |

**Phase 2 — Rewiring (Days 15–45): Build the alternative**

| Day Range | Theme | Science Hook |
|-----------|-------|--------------|
| Days 15–21 | Craving as a wave, not a wall | Urge surfing, 15-minute craving window |
| Days 22–30 | The stress–sugar connection | Cortisol, emotional eating neuroscience |
| Days 31–45 | Substitution science — why alternatives work | GI/GL, dopamine alternatives, habit substitution |

**Phase 3 — Maintenance (Days 46–90): Make it permanent**

| Day Range | Theme | Science Hook |
|-----------|-------|--------------|
| Days 46–60 | Social navigation — the situations that break you | Environmental cues, social identity |
| Days 61–75 | Identity shift — "I'm someone who doesn't need sugar" | Self-concept and behaviour change literature |
| Days 76–90 | Building your permanent system | Habit stacking, environment design |

### 4.3 SOS Session Content Framework

The SOS session is the product's core differentiator. Each session follows a 5-step script that the AI adapts based on user context.

**Step 1 — Acknowledge (0–30 sec)**
> "You opened the app. That's already the hard part."
Name what the user is feeling. Do not minimise.

**Step 2 — Identify (30–90 sec)**
> "What's going on right now? Work stress? Boredom? Social pressure?"
Collect craving context to personalise the next steps and build trigger data.

**Step 3 — Explain (90 sec–3 min)**
> "This craving is cortisol talking. Your team meeting just ended and your body is asking for a fast energy hit. This is biology, not weakness."
Connect to the user's known trigger pattern. Reframe the craving as a physiological response, not a moral failure.

**Step 4 — Act (3–10 min)**
> "Here's what will actually help right now: [specific alternative]. [Why it works in one sentence]."
Provide one specific, context-aware food alternative + one 2-minute physical action (walk, cold water, 10 jumping jacks).

**Step 5 — Bridge (10–15 min)**
> "Most cravings peak at 15 minutes and drop. You're halfway there."
Sustain through the craving window with a micro-distraction (breathing prompt, quick game, short video lesson).

### 4.4 Notification & Push Strategy

Notifications are the primary re-engagement tool. Rules based on research (Kwasnicka 2016 — environment and self-regulation mechanisms):

| Notification Type | Timing | Tone | Example |
|------------------|--------|------|---------|
| Predictive craving alert | 30 min before user's known craving window | Pre-emptive, calm | "3pm is coming. Your plan: green tea + almonds at your desk." |
| Morning intention | 8:00 AM | Grounding | "Day 14. Yesterday you chose water instead. Today: same deal." |
| Slip recovery | Immediately after logged slip | Non-punitive | "One slip doesn't change your streak. The habit is still forming." |
| Milestone | Day 7, 14, 30, 66, 90 | Celebratory, science-linked | "Day 30. Your dopamine baseline has started to reset. It gets easier from here." |
| Withdrawal stage alert | Days 2–5 | Validating | "Day 3 headache is normal. 73% of users feel it. It peaks today and drops tomorrow." |

**Notification limits:** Maximum 2 per day. User controls timing. All suppressible.

### 4.5 "Eat This Instead" Content Engine

The food alternatives feature requires a structured content approach:

**Craving → Alternative mapping logic:**

| Craving Type | Physiological Need | Alternative (tier 1 — ideal) | Alternative (tier 2 — accessible) |

## 5. Key Domain Conclusions for Product

1. **The science supports the concept.** Dopamine pathway activation, withdrawal mechanics, and habit formation timelines are well-documented. The app can make strong, credible neuroscience claims without overstating the clinical evidence.

2. **"Behaviour change," not "addiction treatment."** The safer and more accurate framing. Avoids regulatory risk and the ongoing scientific controversy around the formal "sugar addiction" diagnosis.

3. **66 days is the real milestone.** Not 30 days. The app's primary differentiation should be the 90-day journey, not a 30-day challenge. The 30-day mark should be celebrated but framed as the midpoint.

4. **Slips are statistically normal and must be designed for.** Lally (2010) shows a single missed day does not break habit formation. The streak mechanic must reflect this or it will drive churn at the first slip.

5. **Cortisol → craving is the key insight for Stress Sarah.** This reframe — "it's biology, not weakness" — removes moral weight from the craving moment and is the AI's most powerful opening move in any SOS session.
