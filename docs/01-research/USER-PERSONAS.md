## Persona 1 — "Stress Sarah"

**Tagline:** _"I do great until 3pm, then the office candy bowl wins every time."_

### Demographics

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Age | 29 |
| Gender | Female |
| Location | Chicago, IL |
| Occupation | Marketing manager, 40-person tech company |
| Income | $72K/year |
| Device | iPhone 15, uses ~6 health/productivity apps |
| Relationship | In a relationship, no kids |

### Context

Sarah works in an open-plan office. Her company keeps a communal candy bowl in the kitchen, her team celebrates every small win with baked goods, and there's a Starbucks on the ground floor. She exercises 2–3 times a week, reads nutrition content on Instagram, and considers herself health-conscious — except for sugar.

She has tried cutting sugar at least four times in the last two years. Each attempt lasts 5–12 days before a stressful work week ends it. She knows her triggers intellectually but has no system for the moment they hit.

### Pain (with real quote)

> _"I'm fine all morning. Then a deadline hits, my manager piles on, and by 3pm I'm standing at the vending machine buying a Kit Kat without even consciously deciding to. I'm not hungry. I'm just... done. And then I feel guilty, which somehow makes me eat more sugar."_
> — u/sugarfree_throwaway, r/sugarfree

**Core pain:** Stress-triggered cravings that bypass conscious decision-making. The craving is emotional, not physical. Guilt amplifies the binge cycle. Logging what she ate after the fact (MyFitnessPal) makes the guilt worse without helping the behavior.

### Current Solutions

- MyFitnessPal — logs sugar intake retroactively, but can't distinguish added vs. natural sugar; she finds the warning notifications shaming
- r/sugarfree — reads daily for motivation, occasionally posts, but no structured guidance
- Willpower alone — resets attempt every Monday
- Drinks sparkling water when craving hits — works 30% of the time

### Trigger

A doctor's comment at a routine check-up: _"Your energy dips and afternoon headaches are classic blood sugar spikes. Try cutting added sugar for 30 days."_ She downloaded two apps that same evening.

### Willingness to Pay

- Would pay **$8–12/month** for something that actually works during a craving
- Likely to start with a free trial; converts if she sees a difference within 7 days
- Would NOT pay upfront without a trial — "I've wasted money on apps before"
- Annual plan appeals if framed as "less than one Starbucks run per week"

### Jobs-to-be-Done

| Job | Priority |
|-----|----------|
| **Core job:** Stop me in the moment before I eat sugar I'll regret | Critical |
| Help me identify what I'm actually feeling when I reach for sugar | High |
| Tell me what to eat RIGHT NOW instead of this thing | High |
| Show me that my cravings follow a pattern so I can predict them | Medium |
| Help me navigate the office sugar environment (birthday cakes, candy bowls) | Medium |
| Track my progress in a way that motivates rather than shames | Medium |

## Persona 2 — "Pre-Diabetic Paul"

**Tagline:** _"My A1C result scared me more than anything in my life. I have to change or I'm going to be diabetic in five years."_

### Demographics

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Age | 44 |
| Gender | Male |
| Location | Houston, TX |
| Occupation | Mid-level operations manager at a logistics company |
| Income | $95K/year |
| Device | Samsung Galaxy S24, uses 2–3 apps regularly |
| Relationship | Married, two kids (9 and 12) |

### Context

Paul hasn't thought much about food for most of his adult life. He eats what's fast and available — drive-throughs, office lunches, weekend BBQs. He's 30 lbs overweight by clinical standards but felt fine until his annual physical came back with a pre-diabetes diagnosis and an A1C of 6.1.

He's not a "health app person." He doesn't count calories and doesn't plan to. But fear is a powerful motivator — he watched his father develop type 2 diabetes at 52 and go on insulin at 58. He's looking for something simple that tells him exactly what to change and shows him it's working.

### Pain (with real quote)

> _"The doctor said 'cut sugar and refined carbs' and handed me a pamphlet. That's it. No guidance on what that actually means in real life. I don't know which foods count. I don't know how much is too much. I looked at my breakfast and literally could not tell you if it had sugar in it."_
> — u/prediabetes_worried, r/nutrition

**Core pain:** Lack of specific, actionable guidance from the healthcare system. Fear-motivated but information-poor. Doesn't know what counts as "sugar." Has high stakes (health, family, longevity) but low existing knowledge of nutrition. Needs simplicity above all else — he will abandon anything that requires effort to understand.

### Current Solutions

- Doctor's pamphlet — too generic, ignored after day 3
- Google searches — contradictory information overwhelms him ("Is fruit OK or not?")
- Asked his wife to cook "less sugar" — creates household friction without clear guidance
- No apps currently — "I'm not really an app guy but I'd try something if it was simple"

### Trigger

A1C result of 6.1 (pre-diabetic range). Doctor said: _"If this goes to 6.5, you're diabetic."_ He has a follow-up appointment in 90 days. He wants a number he can show the doctor.

### Willingness to Pay

- Would pay **$10–15/month** if he can see measurable health outcomes
- Motivated by: "Is my blood sugar actually improving?" — outcome data retains him
- B2B opportunity: his company has a wellness benefit program he hasn't used; would use it for this
- Annual plan works if positioned around the 90-day health timeline ("see results before your next check-up")

### Jobs-to-be-Done

| Job | Priority |
|-----|----------|
| **Core job:** Help me understand which foods have hidden sugar so I stop eating them accidentally | Critical |
| Show me concrete health progress (ideally A1C-adjacent metrics) | Critical |
| Give me a simple plan that doesn't require becoming a nutrition expert | High |
| Tell me what I can eat at a restaurant or drive-through — specific, not generic | High |
| Help me explain to my family why I'm changing what I eat | Medium |
| Warn me before I eat something bad (scanner at point of purchase) | Medium |

## Persona 3 — "Challenge Chloe"

**Tagline:** _"I'm doing the 30-day no-sugar challenge from TikTok. I want to document it and actually finish this time."_

### Demographics

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| Age | 24 |
| Gender | Female |
| Location | Austin, TX |
| Occupation | UX designer at a mid-size startup |
| Income | $58K/year |
| Device | iPhone 15 Pro, 20+ apps installed, early adopter |
| Relationship | Single |

### Context

Chloe lives on TikTok and Instagram. She has done a 30-day sugar-free challenge twice before — once in January (lasted 18 days), once in September (lasted 11 days). She followed the challenge because several creators she admires posted "sugar-free transformation" content.

She's not motivated by fear of disease — she's motivated by community, aesthetics, and the feeling of being in a challenge with others. She wants to document the experience, share progress, and cross the 30-day finish line. She already understands nutrition basics; she just needs accountability and social context.

### Pain (with real quote)

> _"I start every sugar-free challenge strong. Day 1–5 is easy. Then day 6 hits and I'm at a birthday dinner or a work happy hour and there's literally nothing I can eat or drink that isn't loaded with sugar, and I cave and I feel like I failed and I just... stop. Every time. I wish there was something that helped me navigate those situations instead of just tracking that I failed."_
> — u/healthchallenge_anon, r/whole30

**Core pain:** Social and environmental situations break the streak. She can handle cravings alone at home; she can't handle the social pressure of a group dinner or a celebration where sugar is the expected norm. Failure at a social event triggers a complete reset, not a "get back up" response. She needs social scripts and pre-event preparation more than craving management.

### Current Solutions

- Streaks app — tracks her sugar-free days; satisfying UI but no guidance when she struggles
- TikTok challenges — community motivation but no personalisation and no in-the-moment help
- Notes app — logs what she ate and how she felt; abandoned after a week
- Instagram hashtags — scrolls #sugarfree content for inspiration
- Has tried MyFitnessPal and found it "too calorie-focused, not sugar-focused"

### Trigger

A TikTok video by a creator with 800K followers: _"I quit sugar for 30 days and here's what happened to my skin, energy, and mood — day by day breakdown."_ 4.2M views. She downloaded two apps within 10 minutes of watching it.

### Willingness to Pay

- Would pay **$6–10/month**, primarily motivated by features she can reference in content
- Likely to pay for a single challenge period (30-day or 60-day) rather than a long-term subscription
- High potential for organic referral / word of mouth — she shares apps she genuinely uses
- Would respond to a "challenge mode" that has a defined start and end date with a community leaderboard

### Jobs-to-be-Done

| Job | Priority |
|-----|----------|
| **Core job:** Help me complete this challenge and cross the 30-day finish line | Critical |
| Give me scripts and strategies for social situations (restaurants, parties, work events) | Critical |
| Show me a shareable progress summary I can post (skin improvements, energy, days clean) | High |
| Connect me with others doing the same challenge right now | High |
| Tell me what to order at a restaurant or what to bring to a party | High |
| Help me get back on track after a slip without feeling like I failed entirely | Medium |

## Cross-Persona Patterns

### What all three have in common

| Pattern | Sarah | Paul | Chloe |
|---------|-------|------|-------|
| Have tried and failed before | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Fail at a specific environmental trigger | Office / 3pm | Restaurant / fast food | Social events |
| Use retrospective tools (logging) that don't help in the moment | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Need intervention AT the decision point, not after | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Would pay for something that works | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Do NOT want to be shamed for slipping | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Want to know "what do I eat instead, RIGHT NOW" | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |

### Where they diverge

| Dimension | Sarah | Paul | Chloe |
|-----------|-------|------|-------|
| Primary motivation | Stress relief / guilt cycle | Health fear / A1C | Challenge completion / aesthetics |
| Tech savviness | Medium | Low | High |
| Social sharing | No | No | Yes |
| Time horizon | Ongoing reduction | 90-day medical goal | 30-day challenge |
| Biggest barrier | Emotional triggers | Hidden sugar ignorance | Social situations |
| Needs most from AI | "Talk me through this craving" | "Tell me what's in this food" | "Help me navigate this dinner" |

### Shared insight for product

All three personas are failed by the same fundamental gap: **tools that track but don't intervene.** Every app they've tried records what happened. None of them help in the 90-second window when the decision is actually being made. The product that wins is the one that shows up at that moment.

## Primary Persona

**Stress Sarah is the primary persona.**

**Why:**
1. **Largest segment** — stress-triggered emotional eating is the #1 cited craving pattern across r/sugarfree, r/loseit, and app store reviews; it dwarfs the other motivations by volume
2. **Highest retention potential** — Sarah's problem is chronic (workplace stress doesn't disappear), so she needs an ongoing tool, not a 30-day challenge app; this drives long-term subscription retention
3. **Strongest product-market fit for the SOS AI** — her core job ("stop me in the moment before I eat sugar I'll regret") is the exact use case the AI craving intervention was built for
4. **Accessible price point** — her $8–12/month willingness to pay aligns with the planned $9.99/month tier
5. **Urban office worker demographic** skews toward iPhone, health-app familiarity, and social media discovery — all matching the planned marketing channels

**Paul** is the highest-value customer (health stakes drive retention and B2B upsell potential) and should be served by the product but is a secondary persona for initial GTM.

**Chloe** represents the viral acquisition channel — challenge mode and shareability — and should inform growth mechanics without being the core retention design target.
